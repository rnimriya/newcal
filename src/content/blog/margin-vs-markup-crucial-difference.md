If you buy a product for $50 and want to make a 50% profit margin, what should you sell it for? If you answered $75, you have fallen victim to the most dangerous math error in retail. 

Adding 50% to your cost gives you a 50% *markup*, but it only gives you a 33% *profit margin*. Understanding the mathematical difference between these two terms is the difference between running a profitable business and going bankrupt.

## Markup: Adding to the Cost

Markup is the percentage you add to your wholesale cost to arrive at your retail selling price. It looks at profit relative to your *cost*.

**Markup Formula:** `[(Selling Price - Cost) / Cost] × 100`

If a shirt costs you $50 to make, and you add a 50% markup:
`$50 × 0.50 = $25 markup.`
Your selling price is **$75**.

## Gross Profit Margin: Taking from the Revenue

Gross Profit Margin is the percentage of your total sales revenue that you keep as profit. It looks at profit relative to your *revenue*. 

**Margin Formula:** `[(Selling Price - Cost) / Selling Price] × 100`

Using the shirt from the previous example (Cost: $50, Selling Price: $75):
`[($75 - $50) / $75] × 100`
`(25 / 75) × 100 = 0.333 × 100 = 33.3%`

When you sell the shirt for $75, your profit margin is only **33.3%**, not 50%.

## The Danger of Confusing the Two

Imagine you are running a business with high overhead (rent, employee salaries, marketing). Your accountant tells you that to stay in business, your overall Gross Profit Margin must be 40%.

You buy a new line of inventory for $100 per unit. Because you confuse margin with markup, you simply add 40% to your cost and price the items at $140. 

At the end of the year, you realize you are losing money. Why? Because a 40% markup on a $100 item only yields a **28.5% profit margin**. You missed your target margin by over 11%, wiping out your entire bottom line.

## How to Calculate the Correct Selling Price

If you know your cost, and you know the target *margin* you need to hit, use this formula to find your selling price:

**Selling Price = Cost / [1 - (Target Margin Percentage / 100)]**

Let's do the math properly for the scenario above (Cost: $100, Target Margin: 40%).
`Selling Price = 100 / [1 - (40 / 100)]`
`Selling Price = 100 / [1 - 0.40]`
`Selling Price = 100 / 0.60`
`Selling Price = $166.67`

To achieve a true 40% profit margin on a $100 item, you must sell it for **$166.67**. (Which is a 66.67% markup!)

## FAQ

**What is a standard retail markup?**
A very common rule of thumb in physical retail (like clothing or home goods) is "Keystone Pricing." This means applying a 100% markup (simply doubling the wholesale cost). A 100% markup always yields a 50% profit margin.
