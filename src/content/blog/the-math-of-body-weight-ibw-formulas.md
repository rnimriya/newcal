For decades, people have asked doctors, "How much should I weigh?" While the answer is highly individualized based on genetics, muscle mass, and lifestyle, the medical community has developed several mathematical formulas to estimate an "Ideal Body Weight" (IBW).

Initially developed to help doctors calculate proper dosages for medications, these formulas have become popular benchmarks for individuals setting weight loss or fitness goals.

## The Big Four IBW Formulas

There is no single universally agreed-upon formula for Ideal Body Weight. Instead, four prominent formulas have emerged over the last century. All of them use a base weight for a person who is exactly 5 feet (60 inches) tall, and then add a specific amount of weight for every inch over 5 feet.

### 1. J.D. Robinson Formula (1983)
This is currently the most widely used formula in the medical community.
- **Men:** 52 kg + 1.9 kg per inch over 5 feet
- **Women:** 49 kg + 1.7 kg per inch over 5 feet

### 2. D.R. Miller Formula (1983)
Miller developed a slightly different mathematical approach in the same year as Robinson.
- **Men:** 56.2 kg + 1.41 kg per inch over 5 feet
- **Women:** 53.1 kg + 1.36 kg per inch over 5 feet

### 3. G.J. Hamwi Formula (1964)
The oldest of the modern formulas, Hamwi's equation was originally used to determine proper dosages for insulin.
- **Men:** 48.0 kg + 2.7 kg per inch over 5 feet
- **Women:** 45.5 kg + 2.2 kg per inch over 5 feet

### 4. B.J. Devine Formula (1974)
Devine's formula is still heavily used in clinical pharmacology to calculate the clearance rates of certain drugs in the kidneys.
- **Men:** 50.0 kg + 2.3 kg per inch over 5 feet
- **Women:** 45.5 kg + 2.3 kg per inch over 5 feet

*(Note: If a person is under 5 feet tall, the formulas simply subtract the assigned weight per inch).*

## The Problem with the Word "Ideal"

It is crucial to understand that in a clinical setting, "Ideal" does not mean "Perfect" or "Healthiest." It simply means "Standardized."

The fatal flaw of every single IBW formula is that they only account for two variables: **Height and Biological Sex**. 

They completely ignore:
- **Muscle Mass:** A 6-foot-tall professional bodybuilder and a 6-foot-tall couch potato have the exact same Ideal Body Weight according to these formulas, even though their health profiles are completely different.
- **Bone Density and Frame Size:** Some people naturally have wider hips, broader shoulders, and thicker bones, requiring them to carry more mass safely.
- **Age:** Natural fat distribution changes as humans age, yet the formulas remain static.

## A Better Approach: BMI Ranges

Rather than aiming for a single, specific "Ideal" number calculated by Robinson or Devine, most modern health experts recommend aiming for a **Healthy BMI Range**.

Instead of saying "You must weigh exactly 160 pounds," the BMI approach says "For your height, anywhere between 140 pounds and 180 pounds is considered a statistically normal, healthy range with minimal risk of obesity-related diseases."

This allows room for individual body compositions and muscle mass variations.

## FAQ

**Should I use IBW to set my weight loss goals?**
IBW formulas are a helpful starting point, but they should not be treated as absolute gospel. A better goal is to focus on lowering your body fat percentage and improving cardiovascular fitness.
