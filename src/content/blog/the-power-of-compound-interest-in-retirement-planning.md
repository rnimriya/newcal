Compound interest is the process where you earn interest not only on your initial investment (the principal) but also on the accumulated interest from previous periods. Over long periods, this creates an exponential growth curve that is the foundation of modern retirement planning and wealth creation.

## How It Works: Simple vs. Compound Interest

To understand compound interest, we must first look at simple interest:
- **Simple Interest:** You only earn interest on your initial principal. If you invest $1,000 at 5% annually, you earn $50 every year. After 10 years, you have earned $500.
- **Compound Interest:** You earn interest on the principal *and* your previous interest. In year one, you earn $50. In year two, you earn 5% on $1,050, which is $52.50. This snowball effect accelerates the longer you leave the money invested.

## The Formula

The formula used to calculate compound interest over time is:

**A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)**

Where:
- **A** = Final amount (Principal + Interest)
- **P** = Initial Principal balance
- **r** = Annual interest rate (as a decimal)
- **n** = Number of times interest is compounded per year (e.g., 12 for monthly)
- **t** = Number of years the money is invested

### Example Calculation

Imagine you invest $5,000 in a retirement account that earns a 7% annual return, compounded monthly, and you leave it for 20 years.

- **P** = $5,000
- **r** = 0.07
- **n** = 12
- **t** = 20

**A** = 5,000 * (1 + 0.07 / 12)^(12 * 20)
**A** = 5,000 * (1 + 0.005833)^240
**A** = 5,000 * (4.0387)
**A** = $20,193.50

Without adding another dime, your initial $5,000 has quadrupled over 20 years.

## Comparison: The Cost of Waiting

The most important variable in the compound interest formula is **t (time)**. Because time is an exponent in the formula, starting early is far more impactful than investing larger amounts later.

Consider two investors, Alice and Bob:

| Scenario | Alice (Starts at 25) | Bob (Starts at 35) |
|----------|----------------------|--------------------|
| Monthly Investment | $300 | $600 |
| Years Invested | 40 years (until 65) | 30 years (until 65) |

*Alice contributed $72,000 less out-of-pocket than Bob, yet ended up with over $150,000 more in retirement, simply because her money had 10 extra years to compound.*

## FAQ

**How often does interest compound?**
Interest can compound annually, semi-annually, quarterly, monthly, or daily. The more frequently interest compounds (a higher "n" in the formula), the higher your final yield. Monthly compounding is standard for many high-yield savings accounts.

**What is a Systematic Investment Plan (SIP)?**
A SIP involves investing a fixed amount (like $300) regularly (e.g., monthly). Combining compound interest with regular SIP contributions is the most reliable strategy for building a large retirement portfolio.

**Does inflation affect compound interest?**
Yes. While your nominal balance grows via compound interest, inflation reduces the purchasing power of that money. This is why investing in assets that outpace the inflation rate is critical.
