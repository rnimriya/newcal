The relationship between speed, distance, and time is one of the most fundamental concepts in classical mechanics. Whether you are trying to calculate how long a road trip will take, pacing yourself for a marathon, or taking a high school physics exam, this single mathematical relationship holds the answer.

## The Magic Formula Triangle

The formula for calculating the physical motion of an object involves three variables:
- **Speed (v or s):** The rate at which an object is moving (e.g., miles per hour, meters per second). Note: In physics, *velocity* includes a specific direction, whereas *speed* is just the magnitude.
- **Distance (d):** The amount of ground the object has covered (e.g., miles, kilometers, meters).
- **Time (t):** The duration of the movement (e.g., hours, seconds).

To easily remember the formulas, visualize a triangle with **Distance (D)** at the top, and **Speed (S)** and **Time (T)** at the bottom.

1. **To find Distance:** Cover the D. Since S and T are side-by-side, you multiply them.
   `Distance = Speed × Time`
2. **To find Speed:** Cover the S. D is over T, so you divide.
   `Speed = Distance / Time`
3. **To find Time:** Cover the T. D is over S, so you divide.
   `Time = Distance / Speed`

## Step-by-Step Examples

### Example 1: Finding Distance
You are driving on the highway at a constant speed of 65 miles per hour (mph) for 3.5 hours. How far did you travel?
- Speed = 65 mph
- Time = 3.5 hours
- **Distance = 65 × 3.5 = 227.5 miles.**

### Example 2: Finding Time
You need to drive to a city that is 400 kilometers away. You plan to drive at an average speed of 80 km/h. How long will the trip take?
- Distance = 400 km
- Speed = 80 km/h
- **Time = 400 / 80 = 5 hours.**

## The Golden Rule: Unit Consistency

The most common mistake people make when using the speed/distance/time formula is failing to match their units.

If your speed is in **miles per hour**, your time *must* be in **hours**, and your resulting distance will be in **miles**. You cannot multiply miles per hour by minutes without converting first!

**Example of the Unit Trap:**
A runner is moving at 10 feet per second. How far do they run in 5 minutes?
1. You cannot do `10 x 5 = 50`. The time units don't match (seconds vs. minutes).
2. Convert 5 minutes into seconds: `5 x 60 = 300 seconds`.
3. Now apply the formula: `10 ft/s x 300 s = 3,000 feet.`

## FAQ

**What is "Average Speed"?**
During a real-world trip, you never drive at a perfectly constant speed. You stop at red lights, accelerate, and decelerate. Average Speed is calculated by taking the *total* distance covered during the entire trip divided by the *total* time taken, ignoring the fluctuations in between.
