Equated Monthly Installment (EMI) is the fixed payment amount made by a borrower to a lender at a specified date each calendar month. EMIs are used to pay off both interest and principal each month so that over a specified number of years, the loan is fully paid off. 

Whether you are applying for a home loan, car loan, or personal loan, understanding how your EMI is calculated is a crucial step in financial planning. This guide will walk you through the EMI formula, provide a practical example, and show you how to easily compare loan options.

## How It Works

The EMI calculation relies on three primary variables:

1. **Principal Amount (P):** The total amount you are borrowing.
2. **Interest Rate (R):** The annual interest rate charged by the lender. (For the formula, we convert this to a monthly rate).
3. **Loan Tenure (N):** The total time you have to repay the loan, measured in months.

The EMI consists of two parts: the principal repayment and the interest payment. In the early years of a long-term loan (like a mortgage), a larger portion of your EMI goes toward paying the interest. Toward the end of the loan, a larger portion goes toward paying down the principal.

## The Formula

To calculate the EMI manually, banks and financial institutions use the standard amortization formula. 

**EMI = [P x R x (1+R)^N] / [(1+R)^N-1]**

Where:
- **P** = Principal loan amount
- **R** = Monthly interest rate (Annual Rate / 12 / 100)
- **N** = Number of monthly installments (Loan tenure in years x 12)

### Example Calculation

Let’s assume you are taking a home loan with the following details:
- **Principal (P):** $100,000
- **Annual Interest Rate:** 6% (So, Monthly Rate **R** = 6 / 12 / 100 = 0.005)
- **Tenure:** 10 years (So, Months **N** = 10 x 12 = 120)

Plugging these values into the formula:

**EMI** = [100,000 x 0.005 x (1 + 0.005)^120] / [(1 + 0.005)^120 - 1]
**EMI** = [500 x 1.81939] / [0.81939]
**EMI** = $1,110.21

Your monthly payment would be **$1,110.21**.

## FAQ

**What happens if I make an extra EMI payment each year?**
Making extra payments (or prepayments) goes directly toward reducing your principal balance. This can significantly reduce the total interest paid and shorten the overall tenure of your loan. You can calculate these exact savings using our [Bi-Weekly Payment Calculator](/loans/biweekly-payment-calculator).

**Does the EMI change if interest rates go up?**
If you have a fixed-rate loan, your EMI will remain the same throughout the loan tenure. However, if you have a floating or adjustable-rate loan, your EMI or loan tenure may increase if market interest rates go up.

**Are there any hidden costs in an EMI?**
The EMI only covers the principal and interest. It does *not* cover other costs associated with homeownership or car buying, such as property taxes, insurance, or maintenance fees.
