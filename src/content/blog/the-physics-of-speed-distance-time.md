If you are driving at 65 miles per hour, how long will it take you to travel 300 miles? 

This is one of the most practical math problems in everyday life. In classical physics, Speed, Distance, and Time are inextricably linked by a simple mathematical relationship called the Kinematic Equation. If you know any two of these variables, you can always algebraically solve for the third.

## The Physics Triangle (The Formula)

To memorize the formulas, scientists often use a visual aid called the "Physics Triangle," with Distance (D) at the top, and Speed (S) and Time (T) at the bottom.

Based on this triangle, here are the three core formulas:

### 1. Calculating Distance
**Formula: `Distance = Speed × Time`**

If you know how fast you are going and how long you have been traveling, you simply multiply them together.
*Example:* You ride a bicycle at **15 mph** for **3 hours**. 
`15 × 3 = 45.`
You traveled exactly 45 miles.

### 2. Calculating Speed (Velocity)
**Formula: `Speed = Distance / Time`**

If you know how far you traveled and how long it took, you divide the distance by the time.
*Example:* You drove **120 miles** and it took you **2 hours**.
`120 / 2 = 60.`
Your average speed was 60 miles per hour.

### 3. Calculating Time
**Formula: `Time = Distance / Speed`**

If you know your destination is a certain distance away, and you know your driving speed, you divide distance by speed.
*Example:* You are driving to a city **300 miles** away at a steady speed of **60 mph**.
`300 / 60 = 5.`
The trip will take exactly 5 hours.

## The Unit Conversion Trap

The mathematical formulas above are incredibly simple, but they hold a massive trap: **Unit Mismatch.**

You cannot multiply Miles Per Hour by Minutes. Your units of measurement must mathematically align before you run the equation.

**The Problem:**
You are driving **60 mph** for **45 minutes**. How far did you travel?
If you just multiply `60 × 45`, you get 2,700 miles. **This is completely wrong.**

**The Solution:**
Because your speed is measured in "Hours," your time must also be measured in "Hours."
You must convert 45 minutes into a decimal fraction of an hour.
`45 / 60 = 0.75 hours.`
Now run the formula: `60 mph × 0.75 hours = 45 miles.`

## Average Speed vs. Instantaneous Speed

When you calculate `Distance / Time`, you are finding your **Average Speed** for the entire journey. 

In reality, you do not drive exactly 60 mph for the entire trip. You stop at red lights (0 mph) and speed up on the highway (75 mph). The speedometer on your dashboard displays your **Instantaneous Speed** (how fast you are moving at that exact millisecond in time).

The formula for Average Speed completely ignores these fluctuations and calculates the mathematical equivalent of driving at one constant speed from start to finish.

## FAQ

**What is the difference between Speed and Velocity?**
In casual conversation, they mean the same thing. In physics, they are drastically different. Speed is a "scalar" quantity (it only has a magnitude, e.g., 60 mph). Velocity is a "vector" quantity (it has a magnitude AND a direction, e.g., 60 mph *North*). If you drive 60 mph in a perfect circle and end up exactly where you started, your speed was 60 mph, but your average velocity was mathematically zero!
