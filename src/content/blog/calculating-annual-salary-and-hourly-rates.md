When you apply for a corporate job, the compensation is almost always listed as an Annual Salary (e.g., $65,000 a year). 

While a flat yearly number is easy to understand, it makes it very difficult to compare job offers if you are moving from an hourly position. If you currently make $30 an hour, is a $65,000 salary a raise or a pay cut? 

To find out, you must understand the mathematical standard of the American work year.

## The 2080 Rule

The entire American payroll system is built around a single mathematical constant: **2,080 hours.**

This number is not random. It is calculated based on a standard, full-time schedule.
- 1 standard work week = **40 hours**
- Total weeks in a year = **52 weeks**
`40 hours × 52 weeks = 2,080 hours.`

Even if you take a two-week paid vacation, your salary is still mathematically based on those 2,080 paid hours.

## How to Convert Salary to Hourly

To find your gross hourly wage, you simply divide your annual salary by the 2080 constant.

**The Formula: `Salary / 2080 = Hourly Rate`**

Let's evaluate that $65,000 job offer:
`$65,000 / 2080 = $31.25 an hour.`

If you currently make $30 an hour, the $65,000 salary is mathematically a raise of $1.25 an hour (assuming you continue to work exactly 40 hours a week).

## The Hidden Danger of Being Salaried

The massive catch to being a salaried employee (specifically "Exempt" salaried employees under the US FLSA) is that you are not paid based on hours worked; you are paid to complete a job. You do not receive overtime pay.

This means your *true* hourly rate fluctuates based on how many hours your boss demands you work.

**Scenario A: The 40-Hour Week**
You make $65,000 and work a strict 40-hour week. 
Your true hourly rate is **$31.25**.

**Scenario B: The 60-Hour Week**
Your boss demands you work late and come in on Saturdays, pushing you to 60 hours a week.
Your new annual hours: `60 × 52 = 3,120 hours.`
`$65,000 / 3120 = $20.83 an hour.`

By working 60 hours instead of 40 on a fixed salary, you mathematically gave yourself a massive pay cut, dropping your true hourly wage down to **$20.83**. This is why understanding the 2080 rule is crucial before accepting a salaried position.

## How to Calculate Bi-Weekly Pay

If you are salaried, you need to know what your actual paycheck will look like before taxes.

Most corporate jobs pay bi-weekly (every two weeks). Because there are 52 weeks in a year, there are exactly **26 bi-weekly pay periods**.

**The Formula: `Salary / 26 = Gross Paycheck`**

`$65,000 / 26 = $2,500.`
Your gross paycheck (before taxes and 401k deductions) will be exactly $2,500 every two weeks.

## FAQ

**Is it better to be paid bi-weekly or semi-monthly?**
- **Bi-weekly** (every two weeks) results in exactly 26 paychecks a year. Because most months have 4 weeks, there will always be exactly *two months* out of the year where you receive a magical "third paycheck."
- **Semi-monthly** (e.g., the 1st and 15th of every month) results in exactly 24 paychecks a year. Your paychecks will be slightly larger, but you will never get a three-paycheck month. Mathematically, the annual total is identical.
