If someone offered you $10,000 today, or $10,000 exactly one year from today, which would you choose? 

Every rational person chooses the money today. But *why*? It's not just about impatience; it's about a strict mathematical and economic principle called the **Time Value of Money (TVM)**. 

Because of inflation (which erodes purchasing power) and opportunity cost (the ability to invest the money to earn interest), a dollar in your hand today is mathematically worth more than a dollar promised to you in the future.

## Calculating Future Value (FV)

Future Value is the exact value of a current asset at a specific date in the future, assuming a certain rate of growth (interest rate).

Whether you are trying to figure out how much a $50,000 inheritance will be worth when you retire in 20 years, or how much a $1,000 CD will pay out in 6 months, you are calculating Future Value.

### The FV Formula

**FV = PV × (1 + r)^n**

Where:
- **FV** = Future Value
- **PV** = Present Value (the amount of money you have today)
- **r** = Interest Rate per compounding period (as a decimal)
- **n** = Number of compounding periods

### Example Calculation
You have **$5,000** today (Present Value). You invest it in a mutual fund that guarantees an **8% annual return**. You leave it there for **10 years**. What is the Future Value?

`FV = 5,000 × (1 + 0.08)^10`
`FV = 5,000 × (1.08)^10`
`FV = 5,000 × 2.1589`
`FV = $10,794.62`

By doing nothing for a decade, the Time Value of Money more than doubled your original investment.

## The Power of Compounding Frequency

In the formula above, we assumed the interest compounded exactly once per year (Annually). However, in the real world, banks often compound interest Monthly, or even Daily.

The more frequently interest compounds, the higher your Future Value will be.

If that exact same $5,000 was invested at 8% but compounded **monthly** instead of annually, the formula changes slightly:
- 'r' becomes `0.08 / 12 = 0.00666` (monthly interest rate)
- 'n' becomes `10 × 12 = 120` (total months)

`FV = 5,000 × (1 + 0.00666)^120`
`FV = $11,098.20`

Simply by changing how often the bank calculates the interest, your Future Value increased by over $300!

## Inflation: The Hidden Enemy

When calculating Future Value for long-term retirement planning, you must account for inflation. If your Future Value calculation says you will have $1 Million in 30 years, that $1 Million will *not* buy the same amount of goods it buys today.

To find your **Real Future Value** (adjusted for inflation), you subtract the expected annual inflation rate (historically ~3%) from your expected interest rate.
If your mutual fund earns 8%, but inflation is 3%, your "Real" interest rate (r) is only **5%**. 

## FAQ

**What is the Rule of 72?**
The Rule of 72 is a mental math shortcut to estimate Future Value. It tells you roughly how many years it will take for an investment to double. Simply divide 72 by the annual interest rate. If you earn 8% interest, `72 / 8 = 9`. Your money will double every 9 years.
