With gas prices constantly fluctuating, fuel is one of the most volatile line items in a household budget. Whether you are splitting the cost of gas with friends for a road trip or trying to figure out if accepting a job with a 45-minute commute is financially viable, knowing how to calculate your exact fuel cost is essential.

## The Fuel Cost Formula

To calculate the cost of a trip, you need three pieces of information:
1. **Total Distance** (e.g., in Miles)
2. **Fuel Efficiency** of your vehicle (e.g., Miles Per Gallon or MPG)
3. **Price of Fuel** (e.g., Price Per Gallon)

**The Formula:**
`Total Cost = (Distance / MPG) × Price Per Gallon`

### Example Calculation
You are planning a 400-mile road trip. Your car gets 25 MPG. Gas currently costs $3.50 a gallon.

1. **Calculate the Gallons Needed:** `400 / 25 = 16 gallons.`
2. **Calculate the Cost:** `16 × $3.50 = $56.`
Your road trip will cost exactly **$56** in fuel.

## The "Cheap Gas" Illusion

A very common psychological trap is driving far out of your way to a gas station that is offering cheaper fuel. Often, the math proves that you are actually *losing* money by doing this.

Imagine your tank needs exactly 15 gallons of gas. 
- Station A is on your route and charges **$3.50/gallon**. Total cost: `$52.50`.
- Station B is 5 miles out of your way (10 miles round trip) and charges **$3.40/gallon**. Total cost at the pump: `$51.00`.

It looks like you saved $1.50! But did you?
If your car gets 20 MPG, driving those 10 extra miles consumed 0.5 gallons of gas. 
`0.5 gallons × $3.40 = $1.70.`

You spent $1.70 in extra fuel to save $1.50 at the pump, resulting in a **net loss of 20 cents**, plus the loss of your personal time. 

## Highway vs. City MPG

When running fuel cost calculations for a commute, you must use the correct MPG rating for your vehicle.

- **City Driving:** Involves constant stopping and accelerating at red lights and stop signs. Accelerating a heavy vehicle from a dead stop burns a massive amount of fuel, resulting in low City MPG.
- **Highway Driving:** Once a car reaches a cruising speed, maintaining that momentum requires very little fuel. Highway MPG is almost always significantly higher than City MPG.

*Exception:* Hybrid vehicles often have a higher City MPG than Highway MPG because they rely heavily on their electric battery at low speeds and capture energy through regenerative braking when stopping.

## FAQ

**Does using the AC reduce my fuel efficiency?**
Yes. Running the air conditioning compressor puts a physical load on the engine, reducing fuel efficiency by anywhere from 3% to 10%. However, driving on the highway with the windows down creates aerodynamic drag, which *also* reduces fuel efficiency. The general rule is: use windows in the city, use AC on the highway.
