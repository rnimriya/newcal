Credit cards are incredibly convenient financial tools when paid off in full every month. However, carrying a balance month-to-month exposes borrowers to some of the highest interest rates in the financial sector, often exceeding 20% Annual Percentage Rate (APR).

Understanding the mathematical mechanics of credit card debt is the first step to escaping it.

## How Credit Card Interest is Calculated

Unlike a fixed-term car loan or mortgage, credit cards are a form of "revolving debt." Interest is not calculated annually or monthly—it is calculated **daily** based on your Average Daily Balance.

1. **Find your Daily Periodic Rate (DPR):** Divide your APR by 365. If your APR is 24%, your DPR is `0.24 / 365 = 0.000657` (or 0.0657% per day).
2. **Calculate Daily Interest:** Every single day, the bank multiplies your current balance by the DPR. 
3. **Monthly Compounding:** At the end of the billing cycle, all the daily interest charges are added to your total balance. Next month, you begin paying interest on the interest.

## The Minimum Payment Trap

Credit card companies usually set the minimum monthly payment incredibly low—typically 1% to 3% of your total balance, plus any interest and fees accumulated that month, or a flat $25-$35 minimum.

This structure is designed mathematically to keep you in debt for as long as legally possible while maximizing the interest extracted from you.

### Example Scenario

Imagine you have a **$5,000 balance** on a credit card with a **20% APR**.
The credit card company sets your minimum payment at **$100 per month**.

If you stop using the card entirely and only pay the $100 minimum:
- **Months to payoff:** 109 months (Over 9 Years!)
- **Total Interest Paid:** $5,840
- **Total Amount Paid:** $10,840

You end up paying the bank more in interest than the original $5,000 you borrowed. 

## The Power of the "Double Payment"

Because credit card interest compounds so aggressively, even a small increase in your monthly payment drastically reduces your total interest and timeline.

Using the same $5,000 balance at 20% APR:
- Paying **$100/mo**: Takes 109 months.
- Paying **$200/mo**: Takes 33 months. (Saves $4,500 in interest!)
- Paying **$300/mo**: Takes 20 months. (Saves $4,900 in interest!)

Doubling your payment doesn't just cut your timeline in half—it cuts it by two-thirds because of how amortization works.

## FAQ

**What is the Debt Avalanche vs. Debt Snowball method?**
These are two popular debt payoff strategies:
- **Avalanche:** You aggressively pay off the card with the highest APR first, mathematically saving you the most money.
- **Snowball:** You pay off the card with the smallest balance first for a quick psychological "win", maintaining motivation.

**Will paying off my credit card in full hurt my credit score?**
No. This is a persistent myth. Carrying a balance and paying interest does *not* build your credit score faster. The best thing for your credit score (specifically your Credit Utilization ratio) is to use the card and pay the statement balance in full every single month.
