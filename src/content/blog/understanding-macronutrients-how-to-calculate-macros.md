While counting total calories dictates *how much* weight you lose or gain, counting macronutrients (macros) dictates the *composition* of that weight. If you lose 10 pounds, you want it to be 10 pounds of fat, not 10 pounds of muscle. This is why athletes and fitness enthusiasts focus heavily on their macros.

There are three primary macronutrients that provide your body with energy (calories): Protein, Carbohydrates, and Fats.

## 1. Protein (4 Calories per Gram)

Protein is the building block of human tissue. It is essential for repairing muscles after a workout, maintaining bone density, and supporting immune function. Protein is also highly satiating, meaning it keeps you feeling full longer than carbs or fats.

**How much do you need?**
- **Sedentary Adults:** The FDA recommends a minimum of 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight.
- **Active Individuals / Weightlifters:** Studies show optimal muscle growth occurs at roughly **0.7 to 1.0 grams per pound** of body weight. 
*(If you weigh 180 lbs and lift weights, aim for ~145g to 180g of protein a day).*

## 2. Fats (9 Calories per Gram)

Dietary fat is often demonized, but it is absolutely crucial for hormone regulation (like testosterone and estrogen production), brain health, and the absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, and K). Because fat is calorically dense (9 calories per gram), it is easy to overeat.

**How much do you need?**
Health experts generally recommend getting **20% to 35% of your total daily calories** from healthy fat sources (like avocados, nuts, olive oil, and salmon). 

## 3. Carbohydrates (4 Calories per Gram)

Carbohydrates are your body's preferred and most efficient energy source, particularly for the brain and high-intensity exercise (like sprinting or heavy weightlifting). While low-carb diets (like Keto) are popular for weight loss, carbs are essential for peak athletic performance.

**How much do you need?**
Carbohydrates fill the remaining calorie gap. Once you have calculated your Protein and Fat needs, you allocate all remaining calories to carbohydrates.

## Step-by-Step Macro Calculation

Let's assume your goal is to maintain your weight, and your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is **2,500 calories**. You weigh **180 pounds**.

**Step 1: Calculate Protein**
Let's aim for 1g per pound of body weight.
`180g of Protein`
Calories from Protein: `180 x 4 = 720 calories.`

**Step 2: Calculate Fats**
Let's aim for 25% of your total calories.
`2,500 x 0.25 = 625 calories.`
Grams of Fat: `625 / 9 = 69g of Fat.`

**Step 3: Calculate Carbohydrates**
Subtract the protein and fat calories from your total to find your remaining carb calories.
`2,500 - 720 (Protein) - 625 (Fat) = 1,155 calories remaining.`
Grams of Carbs: `1,155 / 4 = 289g of Carbs.`

**Your Final Daily Macros:**
- **Protein:** 180g
- **Fat:** 69g
- **Carbs:** 289g

## FAQ

**Does alcohol count as a macro?**
Yes! Alcohol is considered the fourth, "unofficial" macronutrient. It contains **7 calories per gram**, but unlike protein, carbs, and fats, alcohol provides zero nutritional value to the body (often referred to as "empty calories").
