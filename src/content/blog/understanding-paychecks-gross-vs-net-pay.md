Getting your first professional paycheck is an exciting moment—until you look at the final number. If you negotiated a $60,000 salary, you might expect a $5,000 deposit every month. Instead, the deposit is closer to $3,800. 

Where did that $1,200 go? Understanding the complex web of payroll deductions is essential for budgeting and personal finance.

## Gross Pay vs. Net Pay

- **Gross Pay:** This is the headline number. It is the total amount of money you earned before any taxes, benefits, or deductions are taken out. (e.g., $60,000/year or $5,000/month).
- **Net Pay (Take-Home Pay):** This is the actual amount of money deposited into your checking account after all deductions.

## The Big Three Withholdings

In the United States, three major taxes take the largest bite out of your gross pay.

### 1. Federal Income Tax
The United States uses a progressive tax system. This means you do not pay a flat tax rate on all your money. Instead, different "chunks" (brackets) of your income are taxed at increasingly higher rates. 
For example, in 2026, the first ~$11,000 you make is taxed at 10%. The next chunk up to ~$47,000 is taxed at 12%, and so on. Your employer estimates this tax based on the W-4 form you filled out and withholds a portion every paycheck.

### 2. State Income Tax
Depending on where you live, your state will also take a cut. Some states (like California and New York) have high, progressive state income taxes. Nine states (including Texas, Florida, and Nevada) have no state income tax at all.

### 3. FICA (Payroll Taxes)
The Federal Insurance Contributions Act (FICA) is a mandatory payroll tax that funds Social Security and Medicare. Unlike income tax, FICA is a flat tax.
- **Social Security:** 6.2% of your gross pay.
- **Medicare:** 1.45% of your gross pay.
You pay a total of **7.65%** for FICA. *(Note: Your employer is required by law to match this and pay another 7.65% on your behalf).*

## Voluntary and Pre-Tax Deductions

Not all deductions are taxes. You likely have several other items reducing your net pay, many of which actually benefit you.

- **Pre-Tax Deductions:** These are deductions taken out *before* your income taxes are calculated, which lowers your total tax burden. Common examples include Traditional 401(k) contributions and employer-sponsored Health Insurance premiums.
- **Post-Tax Deductions:** These are taken out *after* taxes. Examples include Roth 401(k) contributions or wage garnishments.

### Example Breakdown
Gross Monthly Pay: **$5,000**
- Pre-tax 401(k) contribution (5%): -$250
- Health Insurance Premium: -$150
*Taxable Income is now $4,600.*
- FICA Tax (7.65% of $5,000): -$382
- Federal Income Tax (approx 12%): -$552
- State Tax (approx 4%): -$184
**Final Net Pay: $3,482**

## FAQ

**Why do I get a tax refund in April?**
If you get a large tax refund, it means you instructed your employer to withhold *too much* money from your paychecks throughout the year. The government is simply giving you your own money back (as an interest-free loan you gave them). You can fix this by adjusting your W-4 form with your HR department to get more money in your actual monthly paycheck.
