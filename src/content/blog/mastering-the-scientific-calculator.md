A scientific calculator is an essential tool for high school students, college engineering majors, and professionals alike. Unlike a standard calculator that handles basic arithmetic (addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division), a scientific calculator can handle trigonometry, probability, logarithms, and complex exponential equations.

## Core Functions Explained

### 1. Trigonometry (sin, cos, tan)
Trigonometric functions relate the angles of a triangle to the lengths of its sides. 
- **Sine (sin):** The ratio of the opposite side to the hypotenuse.
- **Cosine (cos):** The ratio of the adjacent side to the hypotenuse.
- **Tangent (tan):** The ratio of the opposite side to the adjacent side.

*Example:* If you need to find the height of a building and you know you are standing 50 meters away and the angle to the top is 30 degrees, you use the tangent function: `Height = 50 * tan(30)`.

### 2. Logarithms (log, ln)
Logarithms are the inverse of exponentiation. They tell you what exponent is needed to produce a certain number.
- **Common Logarithm (log):** Base 10. `log(100) = 2` because 10² = 100.
- **Natural Logarithm (ln):** Base *e* (approximately 2.718). Essential for calculating compound interest and exponential decay in physics.

### 3. Exponents and Roots (x², √, y^x)
- **x²:** Squares the number.
- **√:** Finds the square root.
- **y^x (or ^):** Raises a base (y) to the power of an exponent (x). For example, 2^3 = 8.

## The Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

Modern scientific calculators follow the strict mathematical order of operations, often remembered by the acronym PEMDAS:

1. **P**arentheses
2. **E**xponents
3. **M**ultiplication & **D**ivision (left to right)
4. **A**ddition & **S**ubtraction (left to right)

*Pro Tip:* Always use parentheses when dealing with complex fractions. Entering `10 / 2 + 3` will give you 8. Entering `10 / (2 + 3)` will give you 2.

## FAQ

**What is the difference between DEG and RAD modes?**
DEG stands for Degrees, and RAD stands for Radians. These are two different ways to measure angles. If you are doing basic high school geometry, you almost always need to be in DEG mode. If you are doing college-level calculus or physics, you likely need RAD mode. 

**Why does my calculator say "Syntax Error"?**
A syntax error usually means you have opened a parenthesis without closing it, or you have entered an invalid mathematical operation, such as trying to divide by zero or finding the square root of a negative number.
