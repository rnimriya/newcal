The universe is full of extremes. The mass of the Earth is approximately `5,970,000,000,000,000,000,000,000` kilograms. The mass of an electron is approximately `0.00000000000000000000000000000091` kilograms.

Writing, reading, and doing math with all of those zeros is prone to massive errors. To solve this, scientists developed **Scientific Notation**, a standardized method of writing numbers as a product of a decimal and a power of 10.

## The Rules of Scientific Notation

A number written in proper scientific notation always follows a strict, two-part format:
**a × 10^b**

1. **The Coefficient (a):** This must be a number strictly greater than or equal to 1, and strictly less than 10. (e.g., 5.97 or 9.1).
2. **The Base-10 Exponent (b):** This tells you how many places to move the decimal point. A positive exponent means the number is very large (move decimal right). A negative exponent means the number is very small (move decimal left).

### Converting Large Numbers
Let's convert the distance from the Earth to the Sun: `93,000,000 miles`.
1. Place a decimal point so the coefficient is between 1 and 10: `9.3`
2. Count how many places you moved the decimal point from the end of the original number to its new spot. We moved it 7 places to the left.
3. The exponent is positive 7.

**Answer: `9.3 × 10^7`**

### Converting Small Numbers
Let's convert the size of a red blood cell: `0.000008 meters`.
1. Move the decimal point to the right until you have a number between 1 and 10: `8.0`
2. Count how many places you moved the decimal point. We moved it 6 places to the right.
3. Because the original number was a fraction of 1, the exponent is negative 6.

**Answer: `8.0 × 10^-6`**

## E-Notation on Calculators

If you type a massive calculation into your phone or a standard calculator, you might see a result that looks like this: `3.45E8`.

Because physical calculators and computer keyboards don't always support superscript formatting for exponents (like 10⁸), computer scientists developed **E-Notation**. 

In E-Notation, the "E" simply stands for "Exponent of 10."
- `3.45E8` is exactly the same as `3.45 × 10^8`.
- `1.2E-4` is exactly the same as `1.2 × 10^-4`.

## Calculating with Scientific Notation

The beauty of scientific notation is that it makes multiplication and division incredibly simple by using the rules of exponents.

**Multiplying:** Multiply the coefficients together, and *add* the exponents together.
`(2 × 10^3) × (3 × 10^4)`
- Coefficients: `2 × 3 = 6`
- Exponents: `3 + 4 = 7`
- Answer: `6 × 10^7`

**Dividing:** Divide the coefficients, and *subtract* the exponents.
`(8 × 10^5) / (2 × 10^2)`
- Coefficients: `8 / 2 = 4`
- Exponents: `5 - 2 = 3`
- Answer: `4 × 10^3`

## FAQ

**Why does the coefficient have to be less than 10?**
Standardization. Technically, `45.0 × 10^3` and `4.5 × 10^4` represent the exact same value (45,000). But to ensure that scientists globally write numbers uniformly, the rule strictly dictates that the coefficient must be a single digit followed by a decimal.
