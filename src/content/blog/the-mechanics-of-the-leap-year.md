Everyone knows the basic rule of a Leap Year: every four years, we add an extra day (February 29th) to the calendar. 

But if you assume that the year 2100 will be a leap year just because it is divisible by 4, you are mathematically wrong. The true calculation of a leap year is much more complex, and it all comes down to the physics of Earth's orbit.

## The Astronomical Problem

The foundation of our calendar is the Solar Year (Tropical Year)—the exact amount of time it takes the Earth to complete one full orbit around the sun. 

The problem is that a Solar Year is not exactly 365 days. 
A true Solar Year is roughly **365.24219 days** (365 days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, and 45 seconds).

Because our human calendar only has 365 whole days, we are "losing" about 5 hours and 48 minutes of orbital time every single year. If we did nothing, the calendar would eventually drift completely out of sync with the seasons. After a few hundred years, winter would start in July!

## The Julian Solution: The "Divide by 4" Rule

In 45 BCE, Julius Caesar attempted to fix this drift by introducing the Julian Calendar. He instituted the rule we all learn in grade school: **Add one extra day every 4 years.**

By adding one day (24 hours) every 4 years, the calendar was essentially adding an average of 6 hours per year (`24 / 4 = 6`). 
- True orbit: 365 days + 5h 48m.
- Julian calendar: 365 days + 6h.

*The math was close, but it was too generous.* The Julian calendar was overcompensating by about 11 minutes every year. Over 1,500 years, those 11 minutes added up to a massive 10-day error, pushing the calendar entirely out of sync with Easter.

## The Gregorian Solution: The Exception Rules

In 1582, Pope Gregory XIII fixed the 11-minute problem by instituting the modern Gregorian Calendar. To stop the calendar from overcompensating, he created strict mathematical exceptions to the "Divide by 4" rule.

**The Official 3-Step Leap Year Algorithm:**
1. If the year is evenly divisible by 4, it is a leap year.
2. **EXCEPTION:** If the year is also evenly divisible by 100, it is NOT a leap year. (e.g., The year 1900 was not a leap year).
3. **EXCEPTION TO THE EXCEPTION:** If the year is also evenly divisible by 400, it IS a leap year. (e.g., The year 2000 was a leap year).

Because of Rule #2, the upcoming turn of the century—the year **2100**—will NOT be a leap year, even though it is divisible by 4.

## FAQ

**Will we ever need to change the calendar again?**
Yes, but not for a very long time. The Gregorian mathematical algorithm is highly accurate, but it is still off by about 26 seconds per year. Because of this tiny discrepancy, the calendar will drift by exactly one full day after roughly 3,300 years. Around the year 4900, humanity will likely need to institute another mathematical exception rule!
