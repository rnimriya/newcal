When negotiating a new job offer or considering the leap into freelance consulting, understanding the mathematical relationship between an annual salary and an hourly wage is essential. While "six figures" sounds impressive, dividing that number by the actual hours you work provides a much clearer picture of your compensation.

## The Standard Working Year (2,080 Hours)

To convert an annual salary to an hourly wage, you first need to establish the baseline of a standard working year in the United States.

A standard full-time employee works 40 hours per week.
There are exactly 52 weeks in a year.

**40 hours/week × 52 weeks = 2,080 working hours per year.**

### The Basic Formula
To find the base hourly rate of an annual salary, simply divide the salary by 2,080.

**Hourly Wage = Annual Salary / 2,080**

*Example:* If you make $75,000 a year:
`$75,000 / 2,080 = $36.05 per hour`

## The Problem with the Standard Formula

While the 2,080 rule is mathematically sound, it doesn't reflect reality for most salaried workers. Salaried employees rarely work exactly 40 hours for 52 consecutive weeks. We must factor in Paid Time Off (PTO) and actual hours worked.

### Factoring in Actual Hours Worked
Many salaried jobs demand more than 40 hours a week without paying overtime. If you regularly work 50 hours a week, your denominator changes.

**50 hours/week × 52 weeks = 2,600 working hours per year.**

If you make $75,000 but work 50 hours a week:
`$75,000 / 2,600 = $28.84 per hour`

By working 10 extra hours a week, your true hourly wage plummeted by over $7 per hour!

### Factoring in Paid Time Off (PTO)
Conversely, what happens if you receive 3 weeks of paid vacation and 1 week of paid holidays? You are essentially being paid your full salary while only working 48 weeks.

**40 hours/week × 48 weeks = 1,920 working hours per year.**

`$75,000 / 1,920 = $39.06 per hour`

By utilizing your PTO, the value of every hour you *actually* spend working increases.

## FAQ

**If I'm moving from salaried to freelance, what should I charge hourly?**
You should **not** just use the 2,080 rule. Freelancers have to pay their own health insurance, self-employment taxes (both employer and employee halves of Medicare/Social Security), and account for unbillable administrative hours. A common rule of thumb is to take your desired salary, divide by 2,080, and then increase that number by at least 30% to 40%.

**Is a salary calculated before or after taxes?**
An advertised annual salary is always **Gross Income** (before taxes). Your actual take-home pay per hour will be significantly lower depending on your federal and state tax brackets.
