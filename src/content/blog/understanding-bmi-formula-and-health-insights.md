Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple numerical value derived from an individual's mass (weight) and height. Developed in the 19th century by Adolphe Quetelet, it is universally used by doctors and health organizations (including the WHO) as a screening tool to identify possible weight problems for adults.

## The Formula

The BMI formula is mathematically straightforward. It is defined as the body mass divided by the square of the body height.

**Metric Formula:**
`BMI = weight (kg) / [height (m)]²`

**Imperial Formula:**
`BMI = weight (lb) / [height (in)]² x 703`

### Example Calculation (Metric)

Let's assume a person weighs 75 kilograms and is 1.80 meters tall.

1. Square the height: 1.80 x 1.80 = 3.24
2. Divide weight by the squared height: 75 / 3.24
3. **BMI = 23.1**

## Standard BMI Categories

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), BMI is categorized into the following ranges for adults over 20 years old:

| BMI Range | Category | Potential Health Implications |
|-----------|----------|-------------------------------|
| Below 18.5 | Underweight | May indicate malnutrition, an eating disorder, or other health problems. |
| 18.5 – 24.9 | Normal Weight | Generally associated with the lowest risk of weight-related disease. |
| 25.0 – 29.9 | Overweight | Increased risk of developing cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes. |
| 30.0 and Above | Obese | High risk for cardiovascular disease, high blood pressure, and sleep apnea. |

*Note: These categories are generalized guidelines. Always consult a healthcare professional for a personal health assessment.*

## Limitations of the BMI Formula

While BMI is a useful population screening tool, it has significant limitations when applied to individuals:

1. **Does not distinguish between fat and muscle:** Muscle is denser than fat. A heavily muscled athlete or bodybuilder may have a BMI over 25 (Overweight) despite having very low body fat percentage.
2. **Ignores fat distribution:** BMI does not tell you *where* your body fat is located. Visceral fat (fat around the waist and internal organs) is significantly more dangerous to heart health than subcutaneous fat (fat under the skin).
3. **Age and Sex differences:** Women naturally have more body fat than men at the same BMI. Similarly, older adults tend to have more body fat than younger adults with the same BMI.

## FAQ

**Is BMI accurate for children and teens?**
For children and adolescents (ages 2 to 19), the standard BMI formula is calculated the same way, but the results are interpreted differently. Instead of fixed categories, a child's BMI is compared against percentiles for their specific age and sex.

**What is a better metric than BMI?**
Many modern health professionals use waist circumference, waist-to-hip ratio, or direct body fat percentage measurements (like DEXA scans or calipers) in conjunction with BMI to get a more accurate picture of metabolic health.
