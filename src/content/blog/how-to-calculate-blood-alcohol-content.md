Blood alcohol content — the percentage of alcohol in your bloodstream by volume — follows predictable chemistry. A Swedish physician named Erik Widmark worked out the core formula in the 1930s, and it is still the basis for every BAC calculator and breathalyzer calibration in use today.

## The Widmark Formula

The formula for calculating BAC is:

**BAC = (Alcohol consumed in grams / (Body weight in grams × r)) × 100**

Where **r** is the Widmark distribution factor:
- **r = 0.73** for biological males
- **r = 0.66** for biological females

The difference exists because women typically have a higher proportion of body fat relative to lean tissue, and alcohol does not distribute into fat the way it distributes into muscle and water-rich tissue. Two people who weigh exactly the same but have different body compositions will reach different BAC levels after consuming identical amounts of alcohol.

## What Counts as One Standard Drink?

To use the formula, you need to know how many grams of pure alcohol you consumed. In the United States, **one standard drink contains 14 grams of pure alcohol**. That equals:

- 12 oz of regular beer (5% ABV)
- 5 oz of wine (12% ABV)
- 1.5 oz of distilled spirits (40% ABV)

A 16 oz pint of craft beer at 7% ABV is not one standard drink — it is closer to 1.9 standard drinks. Knowing this matters enormously for accurate calculations.

## How Your Body Clears Alcohol

The liver metabolizes alcohol at a fairly consistent rate regardless of how much you drink, how much water you consume, or whether you eat a large meal. The standard metabolism rate is approximately **0.015% BAC per hour**.

This means that if your BAC peaks at 0.09%, it will take roughly 6 hours for your system to clear the alcohol entirely — regardless of coffee, food, exercise, or cold showers. Those remedies affect how alert you feel, not your actual BAC.

## A Worked Example

A 180-pound male (approximately 81,650 grams) drinks **3 standard beers** over the course of an evening. He wants to know his BAC after **2 hours**.

**Step 1 — Calculate alcohol consumed:**
3 drinks × 14 grams = **42 grams of alcohol**

**Step 2 — Apply the Widmark formula:**
BAC (peak) = (42 / (81,650 × 0.73)) × 100
BAC (peak) = (42 / 59,604) × 100
BAC (peak) ≈ **0.070%**

**Step 3 — Subtract metabolism over 2 hours:**
2 hours × 0.015% = 0.030%
BAC after 2 hours = 0.070% − 0.030% = **0.040%**

At 0.04%, this person is below the US legal limit of 0.08%, but is still measurably impaired. Reaction time and judgment are affected at BAC levels well below the legal threshold.

## Legal Limits Around the World

The legal BAC limit for driving varies by country:

| Region | Limit |
|---|---|
| United States | 0.08% |
| United Kingdom | 0.08% (0.05% in Scotland) |
| Most of the EU | 0.05% |
| Sweden, Norway | 0.02% |
| Czech Republic, Hungary | 0.00% |

Many countries have moved toward or below 0.05%, which reflects research showing meaningful cognitive and motor impairment begins at that level — not at 0.08%.

## Factors That Affect BAC Beyond the Formula

The Widmark formula gives a reasonable estimate, but several variables push results higher or lower in practice:

- **Food in the stomach** slows alcohol absorption, lowering peak BAC but not the total amount metabolized
- **Carbonated mixers** like tonic water or soda speed up absorption
- **Medications** — many common drugs interact with alcohol and amplify its effects at lower BAC levels
- **Fatigue and stress** increase perceived impairment at a given BAC
- **Tolerance** affects how impaired you feel, but does not change your actual BAC or driving ability
