A mortgage is likely the largest financial transaction you will ever make. While the bank determines how much you *can* borrow, understanding the math behind a mortgage helps you determine how much you *should* borrow.

## The Four Components of a Mortgage (PITI)

When people refer to their "monthly mortgage payment," they are usually referring to PITI, which stands for Principal, Interest, Taxes, and Insurance.

1. **Principal:** The portion of your payment that goes toward paying down the actual amount you borrowed.
2. **Interest:** The cost of borrowing the money, calculated based on your interest rate and outstanding principal.
3. **Taxes:** Property taxes assessed by your local government. Lenders usually collect this monthly and hold it in an escrow account to pay the annual tax bill on your behalf.
4. **Insurance:** This includes Homeowners Insurance (which protects against fire, theft, etc.) and potentially Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI). You are usually required to pay PMI if your down payment is less than 20% of the home's purchase price.

## Understanding Amortization

Mortgages are "amortized" loans. This means your monthly payment (Principal + Interest) remains exactly the same for the entire 30-year term, but *what* that payment goes toward changes drastically over time.

In the first few years of a 30-year mortgage, the vast majority of your monthly payment goes entirely to Interest. Very little goes toward reducing your Principal balance.

For example, on a $300,000 loan at 6.5% interest:
- **Month 1 Payment:** $1,896. 
  - $1,625 goes to the bank as interest. 
  - Only $271 goes to paying down your loan.
- **Year 20 Payment:** $1,896.
  - $712 goes to the bank as interest.
  - $1,184 goes to paying down your loan.

This is why building home equity takes a very long time in the early years of homeownership.

## The "Extra Payment" Strategy

Because of the way amortization works, making extra payments early in the loan has a massive compounding effect on your total interest paid.

If you make just **one extra payment per year** (essentially dividing your monthly payment by 12 and adding that small amount to your regular payment each month), you will pay off a 30-year mortgage approximately **4 to 5 years early**.

By doing this on a $300,000 loan, you could save over **$60,000 in total interest payments**.

## FAQ

**Should I choose a 15-year or 30-year mortgage?**
A 15-year mortgage has significantly higher monthly payments, but you will secure a lower interest rate and pay less than half the total interest over the life of the loan compared to a 30-year mortgage. However, many financial planners recommend taking the 30-year mortgage for cash-flow flexibility, and simply investing the difference.

**What are Closing Costs?**
Closing costs are the fees paid to finalize the mortgage (appraisal fees, title search, loan origination fees). They typically range from 2% to 5% of the total loan amount and must be paid upfront in cash.
