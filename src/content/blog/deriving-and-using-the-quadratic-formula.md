In algebra, a quadratic equation is any equation that can be rearranged in standard form as:

**ax² + bx + c = 0**

Where `x` represents an unknown, and `a`, `b`, and `c` represent known numbers, where `a` is not equal to zero. When you graph a quadratic equation, it forms a U-shaped curve called a parabola. Solving the equation means finding where the parabola crosses the x-axis. These points are called the "roots" or "x-intercepts."

## The Quadratic Formula

While some quadratic equations can be factored easily, the quadratic formula is a universal method that works for *all* quadratic equations, no matter how complex the numbers are.

**x = [-b ± √(b² - 4ac)] / 2a**

### Step-by-Step Example

Let's solve the equation: **2x² - 5x - 3 = 0**

1. **Identify a, b, and c:**
   - a = 2
   - b = -5
   - c = -3

2. **Plug into the formula:**
   x = [-(-5) ± √((-5)² - 4 * 2 * -3)] / (2 * 2)

3. **Simplify the discriminant (the part under the square root):**
   Discriminant = 25 - (-24) = 49

4. **Calculate the square root:**
   √49 = 7

5. **Solve for the two possible values of x (using ±):**
   - Option 1 (Plus): x = (5 + 7) / 4 = 12 / 4 = **3**
   - Option 2 (Minus): x = (5 - 7) / 4 = -2 / 4 = **-0.5**

The roots of the equation are **3** and **-0.5**.

## Understanding the Discriminant

The expression underneath the square root, **b² - 4ac**, is called the discriminant. It tells you exactly how many real solutions the equation has before you even finish the math:

| Discriminant Value | Number of Roots | Graph Appearance |
|--------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Positive (>0)** | Two real roots | The parabola crosses the x-axis in two places. |
| **Zero (=0)** | One real root | The tip (vertex) of the parabola touches the x-axis exactly once. |
| **Negative (<0)** | No real roots (Two complex/imaginary roots) | The parabola does not touch the x-axis at all. |

## FAQ

**Why do we need the ± symbol?**
Because a square root has both a positive and negative solution. For example, 3² is 9, but (-3)² is also 9. The ± ensures we find both places where the parabola crosses the x-axis.

**Can 'a' ever be zero?**
No. If 'a' is zero, the x² term disappears. The equation becomes bx + c = 0, which is a linear equation (a straight line), not a quadratic equation (a parabola).
