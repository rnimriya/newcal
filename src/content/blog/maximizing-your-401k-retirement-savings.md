A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored retirement savings plan that allows employees to save and invest a piece of their paycheck before taxes are taken out. Named after a section of the U.S. Internal Revenue Code, the 401(k) is the cornerstone of modern retirement planning in the United States, replacing the traditional pension system for most workers.

## The Twin Powers of a 401(k)

The reason financial advisors urge you to prioritize a 401(k) over standard brokerage accounts comes down to two massive advantages: Tax Deferral and the Employer Match.

### 1. The Employer Match (Free Money)
Many employers offer a matching contribution. For example, a company might offer a "100% match up to 5% of your salary." 

If you earn $100,000 a year and contribute 5% ($5,000) to your 401(k), your employer also contributes $5,000. This is an immediate 100% return on your investment before the market even does anything. *Never leave the employer match on the table.*

### 2. Tax Advantages
Traditional 401(k) contributions are made *pre-tax*. This lowers your taxable income for the current year. If you make $100,000 and contribute $10,000 to your 401(k), you are only taxed as if you made $90,000. The money then grows tax-free for decades. You only pay taxes when you withdraw the money in retirement, at which point you will likely be in a lower tax bracket.

*(Note: Roth 401(k)s operate inversely: you pay taxes now, but withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free).*

## Estimating Your Retirement Goal (The 4% Rule)

How much do you actually need to retire? A popular rule of thumb in the FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early) community is the **4% Rule** or the **Rule of 25**.

According to the Trinity Study, if you withdraw 4% of your portfolio's total value in your first year of retirement, and adjust for inflation in subsequent years, your money has a very high probability of lasting 30 years without running out.

**The Formula:**
`Target Nest Egg = Annual Expected Expenses x 25`

**Example:**
If you expect to need $60,000 a year in retirement to live comfortably:
`$60,000 x 25 = $1,500,000`

You would need a target nest egg of $1.5 million. 

## FAQ

**When can I withdraw money from my 401(k)?**
Normally, you must be 59½ years old to withdraw funds without penalty. Withdrawing early usually results in a harsh 10% penalty fee *plus* regular income tax on the amount withdrawn.

**What happens to my 401(k) if I leave my job?**
The money is always yours. When you leave an employer, you have several options: leave the money in the old plan (if permitted), roll it over into your new employer's 401(k) plan, or roll it over into an Individual Retirement Account (IRA) which often provides more investment choices.
