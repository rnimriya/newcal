If your grandfather tells you that he bought his first house for $15,000, it sounds absurdly cheap. But in 1950, the average annual salary was only about $3,000. 

The physical dollar bill in your grandfather's wallet was mathematically identical to the dollar bill in your wallet today, but it had vastly more **Purchasing Power**. The economic force that slowly erodes purchasing power over time is called Inflation.

## What Causes Inflation?

Inflation is a general increase in the prices of goods and services across an entire economy. It is primarily driven by three forces:
1. **Demand-Pull:** When consumers want to buy more goods than the economy can produce, scarcity allows businesses to raise prices.
2. **Cost-Push:** When raw materials (like oil or lumber) become more expensive, factories pass those costs onto the consumer by raising the price of the finished product.
3. **Monetary Expansion:** When a central bank prints more money and pumps it into the economy, each individual dollar becomes slightly less valuable, requiring more dollars to buy the same good.

## The Consumer Price Index (CPI)

To mathematically measure inflation, the government uses the Consumer Price Index (CPI). 

The government creates an imaginary "basket" of everyday goods and services (milk, gasoline, rent, medical care, clothing). Every month, they check the price of that exact same basket. If the basket cost $100 last year, and it costs $104 this year, the inflation rate is 4%.

## The Purchasing Power Formula

To calculate how much a dollar from the past is worth today, you use a ratio based on historical CPI data.

**Value Today = Past Value × (Current CPI / Past CPI)**

*Example Calculation:*
A movie ticket cost **$0.25** in 1940. The CPI in 1940 was **14.0**. The CPI in 2023 was roughly **304.0**.
What is the modern value of that 1940 movie ticket?

`Value = $0.25 × (304.0 / 14.0)`
`Value = $0.25 × 21.71`
`Value = $5.42`

Adjusted for inflation, that 25-cent ticket was actually the economic equivalent of spending $5.42 today. 

## The Rule of 72 and Savings

Inflation is often called the "Silent Thief" because it steals your wealth without taking any money out of your bank account. 

If you put $10,000 in a safe under your bed for 10 years, you will still have exactly $10,000 when you open it. But if inflation averaged 3% a year during that decade, that money will buy 30% fewer groceries than it could have 10 years ago.

You can use the **Rule of 72** to calculate how fast inflation will cut your purchasing power in half.
Divide 72 by the annual inflation rate.
`72 / 3% inflation = 24 years.`
If inflation averages 3%, the cost of living will double every 24 years, effectively cutting your cash savings in half.

This is why investing in stocks, real estate, or high-yield bonds is critical. Your investments must grow faster than the inflation rate just for your wealth to break even.

## FAQ

**Is inflation always bad?**
No. Most economists agree that a low, predictable inflation rate (around 2%) is healthy for an economy because it encourages consumers to spend money today (stimulating the economy) rather than hoarding cash that will lose value tomorrow.
