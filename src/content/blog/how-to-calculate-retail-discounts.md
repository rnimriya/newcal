Retailers spend millions of dollars studying consumer psychology. They know that a sign reading "30% Off" is much more compelling than a sign that simply lists the final price. However, sale pricing can get complicated, especially when you encounter "double discounts" or "BOGO" deals. 

Understanding how to calculate discounts protects you from clever marketing traps.

## The Basic Discount Formula

To calculate the final price of a single discounted item, you can use two different methods. 

### Method 1: Find the Savings First
1. Find the savings: `Original Price × (Discount % / 100)`
2. Subtract the savings from the price: `Original Price - Savings = Final Price`

*Example:* A $100 jacket is 25% off.
- Savings: `$100 × 0.25 = $25`
- Final Price: `$100 - $25 = $75`

### Method 2: The Direct Multiplier (Faster)
Instead of calculating what you *save*, calculate what you *pay*. If an item is 25% off, you are paying 75% of the original price.
1. `100% - Discount % = Paying %`
2. `Original Price × (Paying % / 100) = Final Price`

*Example:* `$100 × 0.75 = $75`

## The "Double Discount" Trap

Retailers love to offer deals like: **"Take an additional 20% off already reduced clearance items (previously 50% off)!"**

Many shoppers look at this and assume: `50% + 20% = 70% off`. **This is mathematically false.**

Discounts do not stack linearly; they apply sequentially. The second discount is applied to the *already reduced* price, not the original price.

Let's do the math on a $200 television:
- **First Discount (50% off):** `$200 × 0.50 = $100` (The TV is now $100).
- **Second Discount (20% off the new price):** `$100 × 0.20 = $20 savings.`
- **Final Price:** `$100 - $20 = $80.`

You paid $80 for a $200 TV. This is a total discount of **60%**, not 70%! 

## The Math Behind BOGO (Buy One Get One)

BOGO sales are another incredibly effective psychological tool to get consumers to spend more money than they originally intended. 

- **"Buy One Get One Free":** This is exactly the same as a **50% off** sale, but the retailer forces you to buy two items instead of one to get the discount.
- **"Buy One Get One 50% Off":** This is where people get confused. You are paying 100% for the first item, and 50% for the second item. You are paying 150% for two items. 
  `150% / 2 items = 75% per item.`
  Therefore, "Buy One Get One 50% Off" is mathematically identical to getting **25% off each item**. 

## FAQ

**Do I apply a coupon before or after tax?**
Coupons and discounts must be applied to the pre-tax subtotal. You never apply a retail discount to the government's sales tax. Once the discounted subtotal is reached, the local sales tax rate is applied to that final number.
