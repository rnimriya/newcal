Tipping is a deeply ingrained cultural practice in the United States and Canada. Unlike in many European and Asian countries where a service charge is included in the price or servers are paid a full living wage, American servers rely on tips to make up the bulk of their income. 

Whether you're at a restaurant, a salon, or getting a food delivery, knowing how to quickly calculate a tip is a daily necessity.

## Tipping Etiquette: The Standard Rates

While tipping is technically voluntary, there is a strong social consensus on standard rates for good service:
- **15%**: The historical baseline for standard, adequate service.
- **18%**: Increasingly the new baseline in major urban areas.
- **20%**: For excellent service, or the standard default for many modern point-of-sale systems.
- **25%+**: For truly exceptional service, or when asking a bartender or server for extensive customization.

### Pre-Tax vs. Post-Tax
One of the most common debates is whether to tip on the pre-tax subtotal or the post-tax total. 

Strictly speaking, etiquette experts say you only need to tip on the **pre-tax subtotal** (you shouldn't have to tip the server on the money you are paying to the government). However, most people find it easier to just tip on the final post-tax number at the bottom of the receipt. 

## The Mental Math Shortcuts

If you don't want to pull out your phone at the end of a meal, you can use these simple math tricks to calculate a tip in seconds.

### The "Move the Decimal" Trick (Calculating 20%)
To find 20%, you only need to know how to find 10% and then double it.
1. Take your bill total (e.g., **$45.00**).
2. Move the decimal point one place to the left to find 10% (e.g., **$4.50**).
3. Double that number. `$4.50 x 2 = $9.00`.
**Your 20% tip is $9.00.**

### The "Divide by Two" Trick (Calculating 15%)
If 20% feels too high, you can easily calculate 15% using the exact same starting point.
1. Find 10% by moving the decimal (e.g., on a $45 bill, 10% is **$4.50**).
2. Cut that number in half to find 5%. (`$4.50 / 2 = $2.25`).
3. Add the 10% and the 5% together. (`$4.50 + $2.25 = $6.75`).
**Your 15% tip is $6.75.**

## Splitting the Bill

Math gets complicated when multiple people need to split a check, especially if one person ordered an expensive steak and another ordered a side salad.

Instead of splitting the bill evenly, it is much fairer to calculate a "Tip Percentage Multiplier."
1. Decide on the tip percentage (e.g., 20%, or 1.20).
2. Each person multiplies their specific items by 1.20 and adds their share of the tax.

## FAQ

**Do I have to tip for takeout or fast-casual food?**
Historically, no. You only tipped for full table service. However, since the pandemic, "tip creep" has resulted in POS systems asking for 15-20% tips at coffee shops and bakeries. Etiquette experts agree that tipping for counter service is entirely optional, though leaving a dollar or rounding up is appreciated.

**Does a delivery fee go to the driver?**
Usually, no! If you order pizza or use an app like DoorDash, the "Delivery Fee" goes to the restaurant or the app. You must add a separate tip that goes directly to the driver.
