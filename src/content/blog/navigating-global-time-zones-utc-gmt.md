Before the 19th century, every city kept its own local solar time. Noon was simply when the sun was highest in the sky. However, the invention of the railway and the telegraph made local solar time dangerously chaotic. Trains needed standardized schedules. 

To solve this, the world was mathematically divided into 24 standard time zones, creating the complex web of global time we use today.

## The Prime Meridian and UTC

The entire global time zone system is anchored to a single line of longitude: the Prime Meridian, which passes through the Royal Observatory in Greenwich, London.

1. **GMT (Greenwich Mean Time):** The original standard time at the Prime Meridian.
2. **UTC (Coordinated Universal Time):** The modern, highly precise atomic time standard that replaced GMT. While scientifically different, for everyday purposes, UTC and GMT display the exact same time.

All time zones in the world are expressed as positive or negative offsets from UTC.
- New York (EST) is **UTC-5** (Five hours behind London).
- Tokyo (JST) is **UTC+9** (Nine hours ahead of London).

## The Math Behind Time Zones

Because the Earth is a sphere (360 degrees) and rotates once every 24 hours, you can divide 360 by 24. 
`360 / 24 = 15`

This means that geographically, each time zone should represent exactly **15 degrees of longitude**, which equals exactly one hour of time difference.

If you travel 15 degrees East, you move your clock forward one hour. If you travel 15 degrees West, you move your clock back one hour.

### Why It's Not Perfectly Straight
While the 15-degree rule is the mathematical baseline, time zone borders are rarely straight lines. Governments adjust time zone borders to accommodate political boundaries, economic trade areas, and geographical realities. 

For example, the entire country of China spans five geographical time zones, but the government mandates a single national time zone (Beijing Time, UTC+8) for the entire country.

## The Chaos of Daylight Saving Time (DST)

The biggest challenge in converting time zones is Daylight Saving Time (DST).

DST is the practice of advancing clocks forward by one hour during the warmer months. Not all countries observe DST, and those that do often start and end it on completely different dates.

- **North America:** Usually begins DST on the second Sunday in March and ends on the first Sunday in November.
- **Europe:** Usually begins DST on the last Sunday in March and ends on the last Sunday in October.
- **Southern Hemisphere:** Because their seasons are reversed, countries like Australia shift their clocks in the opposite direction at opposite times of the year!

This means the time difference between New York and London actually changes four times a year depending on the exact date.

## FAQ

**What is the International Date Line?**
The International Date Line is an imaginary line located halfway around the world from the Prime Meridian (at 180 degrees longitude) in the middle of the Pacific Ocean. When you cross it traveling West, you add a day to your calendar. When you cross it traveling East, you subtract a day.

**Are there half-hour time zones?**
Yes! A few countries use fractional offsets from UTC. For example, India Standard Time (IST) is UTC+5:30, and Nepal is uniquely UTC+5:45.
